Curate

A conceptual photo de-cluttering app that I developed from concept to high fidelity prototype to help people with overwhelming photo libraries take control of their storage.

The Challenge

The average smartphone in the U.S has 2000+ photos. Ever spent 5 minutes scrolling to find a photo on your phone? Is your Camera Roll filled with screenshots or 20 pictures of of tyring to capture that perfect jumping group photo?

Understand

I needed to learn more about how people currently maintained photos and what frustrations they felt. I interviewed 6 people and analyzed the results, using an affinity map to find patterns in the data.

Findings

Here are some of the highlights from what I learned in the interviews.

Multi-Shot

People often take 3-10 photos of a subject to get the best shot, but then neglect to delete the extras later.

Sharing affects how people remember and feel about photos.

Honestly, anything that I thought twice about, like, this was cool, I'm going to send this to someone else. And I'm sure those are the ones that I'm actually going to remember. – Joe

People used to delete photos when they backed up to a computer, but cloud storage means they have no reason to de-clutter anymore.

I used to delete stuff when I backed up to my computer…now with the iCloud, not so much. – Brenden

Photo Organization Pain Points

Deleting photos is stressful because they are afraid they’ll forget or regret it.

Comparing similar photos is tedious and going through photos is time consuming.

It's hard to compare them on the phone when you're swiping – Ian

Defining the Experience

The interviews and research defined what I wanted the experience to be and guided me when brainstorming ideas.

Empower people to take control of cloud storage.

Make important photos & memories accessible.

Choosing photos to keep is easy and stressless.

Brainstorming the MVP

I narrowed the scope for the MVP (minimum viable prototype) based on difficulty and importance.

One task left behind was “seeing progress”. It felt simple and was left out over risker tasks to test. In retrospect, I should have left this task in for testing…

Prototyping

Usability testing prototypes with real people allowed me to validate and iterate over my designs.

Comparing Photos

Early iterations were complicated and cluttered so I split the actions like swwapping out a photo

Voting on Photos

Surprisingly, voting resonated the most with participants. Participants mentioned several situations like collaborating on albums with friends or getting approval from friends before posting to social media.

Feedback

Giving a little bit of feedback as they whittle down their photos could go a long way. – Jeremy

What I would have done differently

  • Made progress more visible.
  • Thought more about how to show the value.

What I learned

  • Use familiar design patterns when possible.
  • Help the user complete their goals and engage with how they feel while they use it.
  • I am not my user. To me, Curate’s value was obvious but new users need to see how it benefits them.
