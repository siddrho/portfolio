Curate

A conceptual photo de-cluttering app that I developed from concept to high fidelity prototype to help people with overwhelming photo libraries take control of their storage.

The Challenge

Have you ever spent 5 minutes scrolling to find a photo on your phone? Is your Camera Roll filled with screenshots or 20 pictures of of tyring to capture that perfect jumping group photo?

Understand

I needed to learn more about how people currently maintained photos and what frustrations they felt. I interviewed 6 people and analyzed the results, using an affinity map to find patterns in the data.

Here are 3 themes I discovered in the interviews.

1. Rapidfire Photography

People often take 3-10 photos of a subject to get the best shot, but then neglect to delete the extras later.

2. It Means More When You Share It

Sharing photos affects how people remember and feel about them.

Honestly, anything that I thought twice about, like, this was cool, I'm going to send this to someone else. And I'm sure those are the ones that I'm actually going to remember. ‚Äì Joe

Every participant mentioned sending photos to others whether on social media or privately through messaging apps. People are more likely to recall a photo from memory if they've shared it with others.

3. De-cluttering is Stressful

Deleting photos is stressful. People fear if they delete the photo, they might forget the memories tied to them.

The process of decided which photos to delete is also stressful.

It's hard to compare them on the phone when you're swiping. It's much easier to do on a computer. ‚Äì Ian

Comparing similar photos specifically is tedious to do on a phone.

Brainstorming the MVP

After sketching concepts and creating a feature list, I had to narrow the scope to create a minimum viable prototype (MVP). Putting a prototype in users' hands early on means that I can validate my features are what they actually need.

Priority Features

  • Sort similar/related photos together guide cleaning.
  • Suggesting goals and showing progress.
  • Have others help you vote on the best shots.

A few tasks left behind involved seeing your progress and number of space saved. At the time, progress felt too simple and was left out over risker features to test. In retrospect, I should have left this task in for testing because it impacted user's percieved usefulness of the app later.

Prototyping

Usability testing prototypes with real people allowed me to validate and iterate over my designs.

Round 1: Test concept desirability and understanding. Am I on the right track?

Round 2: Test for understanding and usability in mid-fidelity.

Round 3: High fidelity. Fix usability issues and validate improvements.

Findings

Comparing Photos

Early iterations were complicated and cluttered so I split the actions like swapping out a photo


Voting on Photos

Surprisingly, voting resonated the most with usability testers. It was exciting to hear them describe new situations they could have used it like collaborating on albums with friends or getting approval from others before posting on social media.


What was missing?

Giving a little bit of feedback as they whittle down their photos could go a long way. ‚Äì Jeremy

Ah! As I forshadowed in brainstorming, I left out a feature that tracked progress as you used the app. In round 3 of usability testing, I learned that this feature might be more important than I first guessed.

Why does progress matter?

Progress would help to communicate the value of using Curate. For example, it might describe the number of photos deleted, or the amount of space saved on the device. How progress is displayed would have to be tested and validated to ensure it's meaningful to users and helps them feel motivated.

I suspect this was revealed in the high-fidelity prototype because participants could more acurately consider how the app might fit in their lives. Were I to moving forward with Curate, I would consider how to make Curate more valuable.

Next Steps

I would consider the design of the entire app's experience. I prioritized testing a few big features, and missed some opportunities to make the app more engaging. There were also several interesting oppotunities revealed in the voting/messenger features that I would consider researching next.

What I learned from Curate

Lean into familiar design patterns. This is partially why the voting feature was so successful. It resembled something people already use daily and extended it in a intuitive way.

Think about how people feel while using a service. Since working on Curate, I've been facinated about conversational and emotional design.
